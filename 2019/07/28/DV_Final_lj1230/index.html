NYU CUSP Data Visualization - Final Challenge - Junru Lu (lj1230)

Task 1: Serving Rate

  • The average serving rate of whole Manhattan is 55.6%, with around 30% intersections having 100% serving rate but 15% having 0% rate during this 24-hour. Spatially, areas above midtown and below lower Manhattan has higher serving rate than other parts.
  • In the morning rush hour (7AM - 9AM), average serving rate is pretty high: 94.9%; while in the night rush hour (5PM - 7PM), average serving rate drops to 50.4%. This finding is kind of Counterintuitive as I used to think the morning rush hour should be the same as night hush hour. I gusee maybe those who driving cars to their offices are willing to share cars with others in the morning, but urgently go back home after a whole-day hard work.
  • It becomes more harder to match your trips during the midnight (12AM - 3AM), but the spatial character previously listed remains.

Task 2: Analysis of Unmatched Trips

  • The whole picture shows that generally unmatched trips concentrated under 125 street. The more we move south, the denser the unmatched trips.
  • In the morning rush hour (7AM - 9AM), unmatched trips concentrated in the lower Manhattan; while in the night rush hour (5PM - 7PM), unmatched trips are all over the Manhattan. The fact shows that lower Manhattan is the heart of working.
  • During the midnight (12AM - 3AM), there’s even more unmatched trips than the daytime, which truely indicates that the NYC is a Nevernight City.

Task 3: Vehicle Speed

  • In general, almost all speeding cases last very short time and happen at the intersections of main roads. Especially on the East Houston St., Park Avenue, Broadway, Adam Clayton Powell Jr. Blvd and Malcolm X Blvd. I think the reason is that drivers tends to run the red lights at the intersection to save time.
  • From the view of intensity, around 55% cases are minor speedings. However, we still have around 750 severe speedings (>= 25 * 1.5).
  • The most frequent speeding drivers are the owners of vehicle 266, 113, 230, 172 and 368. They are more likely to having speedings on East Houston St. and Park Avenue.

Task 4: Number of Passengers >= 5

  • Carto is not able to visulize all of the exceeding status of all vehicles during the 24-hour day in one map. That’s why I randomly picked 5% of all statuses just for analysis. According to the Law of large numbers, some general trends should be the similar in either sample or the whole datasets.
  • Consistent with the above analysis, due to the highest serving rate, there are fewer exceeding vehicles in the morning rush hour. As time moving to the night rush hour and the midnight, not only more exceeding statuses, but also heavier the average exceeding status. The average number of passengers grows from below 10 to more than 45.
  • From another view, if we limits the range of number of passengers, we can still get similar conclusions raised above. We’ll find that in most cases where the number of passengers eaquals to 5, these exceeding statuses distributes around 5AM to 3PM; and in most cases where the number of passengers more than 20, these exceeding statuses exists in from the late afternoon to the midnight. The results show the NYC is more busier in the night.

Task 5: Number of Passengers <= 4

  • Same logic to show a random sample like task 4.
  • The average utilization of ride-hailing vehicles in NYC is around 82.5% (3.3 / 4). Due to the plenty supply during the morning rush hour, the utilization during that time is only 57.5% (2.3 / 4) and grows to around 90% after night rush hour. Spatially, passagers share vehicles more when they travel in midtown and lower Manhattan, and less above midtwon.
  • Specific vehicles such as 332, 220, 353, 233 and 358 tend to ride more trips, but they do not ride with much more passagers. In contrast, some other vehicles like 466, 406, 21, 247 and 24 tend to ride less trips but more passagers, around 85% of their riding statuses are with at least 3 passagers; and they typically distribute in midtown and lower Manhattan.

Task 8: Vehicle Activities - Take Vehicle 224 as an example

  • This map shows the activities of vehicle 224. There’s some time during the typical day that its trace disappears, which means the vehicle is idling. For the moments it has traces, the faster the vehicle runs the more trips it takes.
